The trek started at 4 AM from Govindghat. 13 kilometers uphill, with the Pushpawati river roaring somewhere below.

## The Ascent

The first few hours were tough. My legs burned, my backpack felt heavier with each step, and the mist made everything damp and cold. But then, around 9 AM, the sun burned through the clouds.

And I saw it.

A valley painted in every color imaginable — blue poppies, pink primulas, yellow marigolds. The flowers stretched as far as I could see, with snow-capped peaks watching over them like silent guardianbs.

> "The Valley of Flowers isn't just a place. It's proof that paradise exists on Earth."


## Day 2: Deeper Into the Valley

I woke up to the sound of birds I couldn't identify. My tent was wet with dew, but the morning light made everything glow.

Today's goal was the glacier at the far end. The trail wound through meadows, crossed small streams, and passed by waterfalls that seemed to appear from nowhere.

## The Meditation Spot

Halfway through, I found a flat rock overlooking the entire valley. I sat there for an hour, just watching clouds drift through the mountains. A Himalayan monal (the state bird) walked by like it owned the place.

Spoiler: It did.

## Practical Tips

1. **Best time**: July to September (when flowers bloom)
2. **Permits**: Required at the forest check post
3. **Stay**: Basic camps at Ghangaria (base camp)
4. **Difficulty**: Moderate. Anyone with basic fitness can do it.

The descent was faster, but my knees felt every step. By evening, I was back in civilization, drinking chai at a roadside stall, already planning my next visit.
